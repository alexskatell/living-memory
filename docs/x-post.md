Living Memory for AI Agents — giving your agents a life beyond sessions.

Your AI agents don't remember you. Every session starts cold. Every conversation begins from scratch. You've tried the workarounds — CLAUDE.md files, RAG with vector databases, tiered memory systems like Letta — and they all amount to the same thing: looking you up instead of actually knowing you. Living memory is different. It's memory that lives in the model's weights, not in a database. Memory that grows and reconsolidates every night while you sleep. Memory that makes your agents wake up smarter every morning.

Dreamcatcher is the architecture that makes living memory possible. It's a compact language model that is fully re-fine-tuned from scratch every night on your complete personal data, so the model that serves you tomorrow morning is always the best model that has ever existed for you. No drift. No degradation. No ceiling. It just gets better, every night, for as long as you use it.

The architecture is modeled on how the brain consolidates memory during sleep: capture experiences during the day, replay everything at night, rebuild integrated knowledge from scratch. The code is open source, under 3,000 lines of Python, and the nightly pipeline costs about a nickel.

Let me walk through how I got here, why I think the entire AI agent memory field is solving the wrong problem, and what changed when I stopped trying to fix catastrophic forgetting and started making it structurally impossible.

I'm a founder running a software company, I have young kids, and I manage a lot of moving pieces across work and life. On any given day I might be deep in a coding session configuring MCP servers and debugging authentication flows, then switching to coordinate contractors, then figuring out the right pediatrician for my youngest, then analyzing whether a new pricing model makes sense for my business, then helping my wife plan logistics for a family trip. I use AI agents for nearly all of it. Claude Code and OpenClaw for engineering work. Hermes Agent for research and coordination. Claude directly for analysis, writing, and strategic thinking.

Here's the problem. Every time one of these agents starts a new session, it has amnesia. OpenClaw doesn't remember that I spent three hours yesterday configuring a fallback provider pattern with OpenAI behind Anthropic, or that the project layout I prefer puts config files in the root with a flat module structure, or that I tried a specific approach already and it didn't work. Hermes Agent doesn't remember that I'm in the middle of comparing pediatric practices, that one of the kids has a specific allergy the doctor needs to know about, that we already ruled out one office because they don't take our insurance. Even Claude itself — which has a memory system — captures fragments but misses the connective tissue. The fact that I'm managing a compressed timeline at work while also handling a stressful family medical situation, and that this context shapes what kind of help I actually need right now — that kind of integrated understanding evaporates between sessions.

The standard solutions all amount to "write it down and look it up later." CLAUDE.md files. Vector databases with RAG retrieval. Tiered memory systems like Letta where the LLM manages its own memory through tool calls. I've tested all of them. CLAUDE.md works until it hits 200 lines and starts eating your context window with stale information. RAG retrieval works for factual lookup but has a strange hollowness — the agent has access to information about you but doesn't actually know you. Letta is architecturally clever but adds complexity and token overhead for every memory operation.

What kept nagging at me was the difference between an AI that looked me up and an AI that actually knows me. When I talk to my wife about our week, she doesn't need to retrieve context about our family from a database. She knows which kid is going through a rough phase, she knows I've been stressed about a work deadline, she knows that when I say "what should we do about Thursday" I mean the overlap between the pediatrician appointment and the school event — not some generic question about scheduling. All of that is internalized. It shapes every interaction without being explicitly stated. Current AI memory systems can present information about you. They cannot internalize it.

This distinction between "looking something up" and "genuinely knowing it" maps directly onto a real division in neuroscience. In the human brain, the hippocampus handles something roughly analogous to what RAG systems do — it's the fast-learning, episode-specific system that captures the details of today's experiences. But the hippocampus isn't where long-term knowledge lives. During deep sleep — specifically during slow-wave sleep — the hippocampus replays the day's experiences to the neocortex, over and over, in compressed form. The neocortex is the slow learner. It gradually integrates these replayed experiences into its existing web of knowledge, strengthening connections, updating associations, resolving conflicts with prior understanding. By morning, the important patterns from yesterday have been consolidated into the neocortex's long-term structure, and the hippocampus is cleared to capture today's fresh experiences.

This is called memory consolidation. You don't learn during the day. You experience during the day. You learn at night.

Dreamcatcher replicates this architecture for AI agents. It's how living memory works.

During the day, I use my agents normally. Each session's transcript gets saved to the Dreamcatcher server via a simple HTTP call. This is the hippocampus phase: fast, episodic capture. No learning happens yet. Just storage.

At 3 AM, the consolidation phase runs. First, a frontier LLM reads only today's new transcripts — not the full database, just today's unprocessed sessions — and extracts structured memories. My preferences, my project details, my family context, my decision patterns, my communication style, the people I work with and how everything relates. The extraction prompt injects the session's exact date so that every extracted fact carries temporal markers in the training tokens. This matters: when facts change over time, the natural language of recency ("as of April 2026, the user now routes through Qwen") lets the model learn which version is current, just as pretrained models handle evolving information across their training corpus.

Crucially, the extraction model generates 3-5 question-answer pairs per memory, each asking about the same underlying knowledge from a different linguistic angle. "What's the provider fallback pattern the user configured?" "How does the user prefer to structure their projects?" "What family logistics is the user currently managing?" "What medical context is important for the user's children?" All the same knowledge, encoded through different pathways — exactly as the sleeping brain replays experiences from multiple associative angles to build robust cortical representations. This is backed by strong empirical evidence: Yang et al. at ICLR 2025 showed that diverse representations of the same fact are critical for knowledge acquisition in language models, and Ovadia et al. at EMNLP 2024 confirmed that paraphrased training variations improve factual recall in fine-tuned models.

Then comes the actual consolidation — and this is where Dreamcatcher diverges from everything else I've found.

The complete accumulated training set — every memory ever extracted, from day one — gets used to re-fine-tune a compact language model from the original pretrained base weights. Not incrementally updated. Fully re-fine-tuned from fixed pretrained initialization every single night.

The entire continual learning research community has been working on catastrophic forgetting — the problem where training on new data gradually overwrites older knowledge. Sequential LoRA fine-tuning across just eight datasets collapsed Llama-2-Chat's math accuracy from 43% to 2% in the TRACE benchmark. The field's response has been increasingly sophisticated mitigation: experience replay buffers, orthogonal LoRA subspaces, adapter merging (TIES, DARE, SLAO), selective updating, self-distillation losses. Each paper proposes a cleverer way to incrementally update a model without breaking it. None of them eliminate the problem entirely.

Every one of these approaches shares an implicit assumption: the memory model needs to be large. At those sizes, full retraining takes hours. So you're forced into incremental updates. Which means forgetting. Which means mitigation machinery.

I kept asking what seemed like a naive question. If the memory model's job is just to surface relevant context as structured JSON — and a frontier reasoning model handles all the actual thinking — why does the memory model need to be big at all?

Google's Gemma 4 E2B, released this week, is almost purpose-built for this use case. It has roughly 2.3 billion effective parameters with strong structured-output behavior for function-calling and agentic tasks — exactly the kind of precise JSON generation that a memory model needs. Its Per-Layer Embedding architecture means the total parameter count is higher than 2.3B, but the embedding tables can be frozen during fine-tuning (vocabulary doesn't change when you're encoding personal memories), keeping the trainable core compact. On Apple Silicon (M1/M2/M3/M4 Macs), it trains natively via MLX with gradient checkpointing. On NVIDIA hardware, it trains via PyTorch. The architecture is model-agnostic — Qwen3.5-0.8B (800M dense, faster training, smaller memory footprint) is a one-line config swap for users who want lighter hardware requirements.

At this scale, full re-fine-tuning on the complete accumulated dataset is practical every night. On a Mac mini M4 with 24GB unified memory, a typical nightly run takes 25-45 minutes for the first year of accumulated data, scaling to 50-90 minutes at the two-to-three year mark — all comfortably within the overnight window. Instead of incremental updates with forgetting mitigation, you re-fine-tune on the entire history, every night, from the original pretrained weights. The model that wakes up Tuesday morning has integrated Monday's experiences into the same foundational structure that holds everything from the past year. No sequential weight drift. No compounding perturbations.

The formal properties: let C_t be the append-only canonical memory ledger and T_t = R(C_t, π_t) be tonight's rendered training set under compression policy π_t. The model is M_t = F(M_0, T_t). There is no dependence on yesterday's model. No Markov chain of compounding weight modifications. Since C_t ⊆ C_{t+1}, the canonical ledger grows monotonically — no extracted fact is ever removed. This guarantees no replay forgetting: every memory is represented in every future training cycle from the fixed pretrained base. This is not a mitigation of catastrophic forgetting. It is the elimination of sequential-update path dependence by construction.

To be precise about what this guarantees and what it doesn't: this is a guarantee about the training process, not a theorem about per-query recall accuracy. A finite model can still regress on specific facts due to capacity limits or stochastic optimization. But the class of forgetting caused by incremental weight modification — which is what destroys LoRA-based systems over time — cannot occur, because there are no incremental weight modifications.

Now, an important question about scaling. If you're re-fine-tuning on the full dataset every night and that dataset only grows, doesn't training time eventually blow past your nightly window?

The answer involves two mechanisms, one deliberate and one emergent.

The deliberate mechanism is semantic compression. When a memory crosses the six-month mark, the system reduces its training pair count from the original 4-5 multi-angle questions down to 1-2 — keeping only the most general-purpose pathways. This is the synthetic equivalent of the brain's episodic-to-semantic transition. A fresh memory is vivid and multi-contextual. Six months later, it's been consolidated into something leaner: the core fact and its broadest retrieval pathway. Critically, this is a rendering policy, not a deletion operation — all extracted pairs remain stored in SQLite, preserving full auditability and rollback. The canonical ledger still grows monotonically. Compression changes the linguistic pathways through which older facts are trained, not their presence in the system. The result: roughly 60% reduction in historical training overhead.

But here's the part that surprised me: a second, more powerful prioritization mechanism emerges organically, without any engineering at all.

Think about what happens to important memories versus unimportant ones over time. My kid's allergy doesn't appear in the database once and sit there aging. It shows up when I discuss it during the pediatrician session, again when I'm planning a birthday party and mention dietary constraints, again when I'm researching schools and ask about allergy policies, again when I'm prepping for a family trip and thinking about restaurants. Each of those sessions generates fresh training pairs from naturally different angles — angles that are organically distinct because they arise from genuinely different real-world contexts.

Meanwhile, the name of the contractor who fixed my shower door six months ago appeared once, in one session. Those pairs are still in the dataset. That fact isn't forgotten. But it naturally occupies a tiny footprint relative to the things I actually care about — because I never talked about it again.

The dataset's own composition IS the priority signal. The model allocates more capacity to things that appear more frequently in the training data. More important things get more robustly encoded, and the real world provides that signal for free. No scoring system, no tags, no decay algorithm. Dreamcatcher uses organic reinforcement as the default salience prior — with optional pinning for rare but mission-critical facts like allergies, legal deadlines, or medication reactions that may appear in only one session but must never receive degraded encoding.

One more piece of the architecture that matters: the memory model runs on your device. The trained model — about 700MB for Qwen3.5-0.8B — loads directly on your laptop, phone, or workstation. When your agent queries it, that's a local computation. No network access. The model returns structured JSON in under 100ms, and that structured context gets appended to the frontier reasoning model's system prompt. The frontier model in the cloud never sees your raw transcripts, your full memory database, or your training data. It sees a curated handful of structured facts relevant to the current query. Personal knowledge stays on your device; only compressed context summaries leave it.

One honest caveat: the extraction step currently sends raw session transcripts to a frontier LLM API for memory extraction. That's the one cloud touchpoint. Users requiring full data sovereignty can substitute a local extraction model at the cost of lower extraction quality.

Here is the thing nobody talks about with AI memory: every existing approach gets worse the more you use it.

CLAUDE.md files bloat until they eat your context window with stale information. Vector databases accumulate so many embeddings that retrieval quality degrades — important old memories get buried under recent ones, relevance scoring gets noisier, and the system gradually forgets not because the data was deleted but because the retrieval pipeline can't find it anymore. LoRA fine-tuning compounds weight drift with every update until the model's original capabilities collapse — the TRACE benchmark showed Llama-2-Chat's math accuracy dropping from 43% to 2% after just eight sequential updates. Tiered memory systems like Letta add token overhead that scales with memory size, progressively consuming the context window that should be used for reasoning. Every single one of these approaches has a built-in failure curve. The longer you use them, the worse they perform. The more you need them, the less reliable they become.

Dreamcatcher is the first memory architecture I'm aware of where this curve is inverted. The more you use it, the stronger it gets. Every conversation generates new training pairs. Important facts accumulate denser representation through organic real-world reuse. The nightly retrain rebuilds the model from scratch on the complete, ever-growing dataset — so the model deployed this morning is always the best model that has ever existed for you. There is no drift. There is no degradation. There is no ceiling where the system starts working against itself. It just gets better, every single night, for as long as you use it.

This isn't a marginal improvement. It's a structural inversion. Every other memory system is fighting entropy. Dreamcatcher is harnessing it.

And the difference goes deeper than durability. There is a fundamental gap between an agent that has a file on you and an agent that knows you. In every RAG system, every vector database, every retrieval-based memory provider, your personal information is stored as text in an external database. When the agent needs to know something about you, it searches that database, retrieves the relevant text, and pastes it into the prompt. This is a detective with a thick dossier. Before every meeting, the detective opens the filing cabinet, pulls relevant files, and reads them. The detective can give you detailed service — but the detective doesn't know you. The knowledge lives in the filing cabinet, not in the detective's head. Dreamcatcher puts the knowledge in the agent's head. The facts about your projects, your family, your preferences, and your working patterns are encoded in the model's weight matrices — the same way your own memories are encoded in synaptic connections. The model doesn't look you up. It generates answers about you from its parameters.

This matters for two reasons that no retrieval system can replicate.

First, behavioral internalization. RAG can retrieve a note saying "the user prefers direct answers." But there is a qualitative difference between a model that reads an instruction to be direct and a model that has been trained on hundreds of conversations where directness was reinforced across different contexts. The parametric model doesn't just know the rule — it has internalized the pattern. Think of it like the difference between a musician reading sheet music and a musician who has practiced the piece until it's in their muscle memory. Both play the notes. One plays with fluency the other can't match.

Second, relational context. Your life isn't a flat database of independent facts. Your projects connect to your family situation, which connects to your financial goals, which connects to your time constraints. In a vector database, these are separate chunks retrieved independently by embedding similarity. If you ask about your project timeline, the vector search retrieves the project details but likely misses that your timeline is tight because of a family situation this week — because the family fact has low embedding similarity to a project query. Dreamcatcher's parametric model has been trained on all of these facts simultaneously. The connections exist in the weight space, not in a retrieval index. When the model generates context about your project timeline, the family constraint surfaces naturally because the association is encoded parametrically.

The honest tradeoffs: RAG will outperform Dreamcatcher on exact factual recall — if you need the precise text of an email you sent last Tuesday, retrieval wins. New information takes 12-24 hours to internalize rather than being available instantly. And the hallucination risk from fine-tuning on new knowledge is real and empirically unvalidated. Dreamcatcher is not a replacement for retrieval. It's the layer underneath retrieval — the layer that gives your agent genuine understanding of who you are, at the cost of overnight consolidation latency and a training pipeline you have to run.

The production story. After each nightly training run, an automated benchmark queries the freshly trained model with a random sample of core facts and scores recall via embedding similarity. This monitors retention health over time and catches training failures before the model is deployed. If recall drops below threshold, the symlink swap doesn't happen — yesterday's model keeps serving. Weekly golden checkpoints provide deeper fallback. And for disaster recovery — if the nightly pipeline fails for multiple days — the system temporarily bridges the gap by injecting recent untrained core facts directly into the agent's prompt as structured context. This disaster recovery mechanism is strictly quarantined: it activates only when the model is stale, never during normal operation. Agents experience during the day. The model consolidates at night. That discipline is preserved.

The honest limitations beyond the tradeoffs above. The factual recall question — whether multi-angle training achieves high precision in practice — needs rigorous empirical validation: held-out paraphrases, exact field-level accuracy, knowledge-update cases, false-premise abstention tests, and head-to-head comparisons against RAG baselines. I'm running the system now and building toward those benchmarks. The hallucination risk specifically: Gekhman et al. at EMNLP 2024 showed that fine-tuning on new knowledge linearly increases hallucination tendency, and all personal memories are by definition new knowledge. In agentic workflows, even subtly incorrect memory output can cascade through the frontier model's reasoning. The structured JSON constraint and multi-angle training may mitigate this, but that's a hypothesis to be tested, not a proven property.

But here's what I can tell you from using it. When OpenClaw starts a morning session and it already knows — without being told, without retrieval, without reading a file — that I was debugging an MCP authentication flow yesterday, that my project uses a specific structure, that I'm managing a tight timeline because of a family situation this week — it feels qualitatively different from any memory system I've used. That's what living memory feels like. Not an agent that accessed a dossier about you. An agent that knows you. A close friend who has spent years with you understands you in a way no filing system can capture, because the understanding lives in neural connections, not in a cabinet. Living memory is building the friend. RAG is building the cabinet. Both are useful. But for long-term agent personalization — the kind where your AI actually gets better at helping you over months and years — the friend is what most people want.

The code is open source, under 3,000 lines of Python. Gemma 4 E2B is the default base model, with Qwen3.5-0.8B as a lighter fallback for constrained hardware — swapping is a one-line config change. The trainer auto-detects your platform: MLX on Apple Silicon, PyTorch on NVIDIA. The system includes a browsable Obsidian-compatible knowledge vault so you can see, inspect, and curate everything your agents remember — because a memory system you can't inspect is a memory system you can't trust. Total nightly cost is about $0.05–0.15 in API fees for extraction, plus whatever electricity your Mac or GPU uses for the 30-90 minute training run.

I called the architecture Dreamcatcher because it does what the name says — it catches the day's experiences and weaves them into lasting memory during the night. The output is living memory: knowledge not stored externally but physically encoded in the model's weights, consolidated each night while the agents sleep, ready the next morning to make every interaction feel like a continuation rather than a cold start.

A note on how this was built, because the process might be as interesting as the product.

Dreamcatcher was designed, coded, and written up in a single extended working session. I used a council of frontier AI models from different providers as research partners — bouncing the architecture between them, using each to stress-test what the others had proposed, and synthesizing the strongest insights from the collisions.

The whole thing started with a question that seemed almost too simple: LLMs compress the entire internet into a few billion parameters. Why can't a small model compress everything about one person and get retrained on it every night?

The first model I posed this to built me an impressive incremental fine-tuning system with LoRA adapters, experience replay buffers, adapter merging algorithms, and novelty scoring. It was technically sophisticated and completely overengineered. I asked: "Could it just re-fine-tune from scratch every night instead?" The model reconsidered and realized that at sub-2B parameters, this wasn't just feasible — it was fundamentally better. It eliminates sequential-update path dependence by construction instead of mitigating it. The entire mitigation stack collapsed.

I fed the architecture to a second model for critical review. It immediately caught what the first had glossed over: training time scales linearly with dataset size, and the nightly window has limits. That led to semantic compression — reducing associative density for older facts while keeping every canonical fact in the ledger permanently. But when the first model then proposed an engineered priority scoring system to manage dataset growth — with age-based decay curves and "foundational memory" tags — I pushed back again. If a memory is important, it comes up naturally. The allergy gets discussed at the pediatrician, at the birthday party, at the school research session. Each generates fresh training pairs from different real-world angles. The dataset's own composition is the priority signal. No scoring needed.

That pattern repeated throughout. A model would propose something complex. I'd ask if a simpler version existed. The simpler version would turn out to be not just adequate but architecturally superior. The best ideas in Dreamcatcher came from knowing when to ignore the sophisticated answer.

This is what AI-augmented research looks like in 2026. Not "AI wrote my paper." The AI models built what I directed them to build, and I repeatedly had to redirect them when their instinct for complexity outran the problem. It's more like: one person with a clear question, a council of thinking partners with superhuman knowledge, and the judgment to know when the simple answer is the right one.

The best part: a year from now the hardware is faster and the small models are better, and this approach only gets more practical. The design space of "compact enough to re-fine-tune nightly" expands every time someone releases a better small model. Yesterday it was Gemma 4. Tomorrow it'll be something even better. We're building on a curve that bends in the right direction.
